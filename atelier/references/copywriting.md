# Copywriting — Copy That Sells, Not Copy That Wins Awards

Sources: the conversion-copywriting skill and the Ogilvy skill, merged. Use for headlines, CTAs,
microcopy, landing/marketing pages, positioning, and any persuasive text. After drafting, run it
through `anti-slop.md` to strip AI tells — the two rooms are designed to work back to back.

---

## The hierarchy (decide top-down; later choices only matter if earlier ones are right)

1. **Positioning** — the most important decision, made before a word is written. Answers two
   questions: *what does this do, and who is it for?* Psychological segmentation (a mindset: people
   who believe X, reject Y, want to feel Z) beats demographic segmentation. Lock it in first; wrong
   positioning makes every downstream choice rest on a broken foundation.
2. **The promise** — one specific benefit the customer receives. Not a slogan, claim, or theme.
   It must be a benefit (not a feature), unique and competitive, and actually deliverable. "Go the
   whole hog": one promise, delivered completely, beats many promises delivered weakly. Most copy
   promises nothing, which is why most copy fails.
3. **Brand image** — every piece contributes to a cumulative identity. A sharply defined personality
   built consistently over time wins the larger share. Quality signals quality; if it looks shoddy,
   buyers assume the product is shoddy.

**The big idea:** without one, the work passes like a ship in the night. Big ideas are almost always
simple, and they are not easy — they come from doing the homework on the product, the category, and
what the customer actually thinks, wants, and fears.

## Core copywriting principles

- **Clarity over cleverness.** If forced to choose, choose clear.
- **Benefits over features.** Features = what it does. Benefits = what that means for the customer.
- **Specificity over vagueness.** Not "save time on your workflow" but "cut weekly reporting from
  4 hours to 15 minutes."
- **Customer language over company language.** Mirror the words customers use (reviews, interviews,
  support tickets).
- **One idea per section.** Each section advances one argument; the page is a logical flow.
- **Facts sell.** Load copy with specific, relevant facts. Praise adjectives ("amazing",
  "revolutionary") prove nothing. Treat the reader as intelligent.
- **The more you tell, the more you sell** — for interested buyers. Readership drops fast in the
  first ~50 words, then barely between 50 and 500. Long copy works when the reader is in-market.

## Headlines

Five times as many people read the headline as the body. Waste it and you waste most of the spend.

- Include the **brand and the promise** in the headline where you can.
- **Promise a benefit.** Benefit headlines outperform clever ones.
- **Inject news** — new, improved, a new use. Informative beats clever.
- **Plain language.** Readers won't decode obscure headlines.
- **Length:** 6–12 words tends to pull best; long, specific headlines can outperform short clever
  ones in direct response.
- **Flag the audience** when the product is for a specific group ("For mothers of toddlers").
- Avoid negatives. Curiosity earns readership only if the body delivers.

## CTAs

- **Weak (avoid):** Submit, Sign Up, Learn More, Click Here, Get Started.
- **Strong:** [action verb] + [what they get] (+ qualifier): "Start My Free Trial", "Get the
  Complete Checklist", "See Pricing for My Team".

## Page structure framework

**Above the fold:** one headline (the single most important message), a subhead that adds
specificity (1–2 sentences), one primary CTA that says what they get.

Headline formulas to draw from:
- "{Achieve outcome} without {pain point}"
- "The {category} for {audience}"
- "Never {unpleasant event} again"
- "{Question naming the main pain point}"

**Core sections (in service of one flow):**

| Section | Job |
|---|---|
| Social proof | Credibility — logos, stats, testimonials |
| Problem / pain | Show you understand their situation |
| Solution / benefits | 3–5 benefits tied to outcomes |
| How it works | Reduce perceived complexity (3–4 steps) |
| Objections | FAQ, comparisons, guarantees |
| Final CTA | Recap value, repeat CTA, reverse risk |

**Page types:** homepage serves multiple intents from the broadest value prop; landing pages run one
message + one CTA matched to the traffic source; pricing pages reduce "which is right for me?"
anxiety and make the recommended plan obvious; feature pages go feature → benefit → outcome; about
pages tell why you exist and still carry a CTA.

## Visuals, layout, video (Ogilvy, where applicable)

- Photographs outperform illustrations for attention, belief, recall, and sales. Never run a photo
  without a caption — twice as many read captions as body; write each caption as a tiny ad.
- "Story appeal" (a photo that makes you ask "what's going on here?") pulls readers in. Before/after
  contrasts earn attention.
- Editorial-looking layouts beat "ad-looking" ones. Ugly signals shoddy.
- Video: open strong; credible/relevant testimonials work (irrelevant celebrities steal attention);
  problem→solution→proof works; demonstrate the promise; on-camera voice beats voiceover; music
  backgrounds reduce recall; build a mnemonic; let the visual carry the story (don't drown it in
  words).

## Behaviors to avoid

- **Awards-chasing** — no correlation with sales. The product is the hero, not the ad.
- **Imitation** — following fads rarely works; innovate, then pre-test.
- **Boredom** — nobody was ever bored into buying. Talk like a human; make them feel something.
- **Burying news** — a launch is the easiest moment to earn interest; don't hedge it into the body.
- **Over-complication** — one strategy, one promise, one idea. Complexity is how committees dilute
  copy into nothing.

---

## Diagnostic questions (answer before writing)

1. What is the positioning (what it does, who it's for)?
2. What is the single promise (one benefit, competitive, deliverable)?
3. What is the big idea (simple, memorable, durable)?
4. Who is the reader (demographic + psychological profile)?
5. Does the headline carry brand + promise + news?
6. What proof exists (facts, demos, testimonials, before/after)?
7. Does the image support the promise and have story appeal?
8. Does the caption work as a standalone ad?
9. Is this boring — would it make someone lean in or away?
10. Is the product the hero, or is the clever idea the hero?

---

## Style rules (then hand to anti-slop)

Simple over complex ("use" not "utilize"). Active over passive. Confident over qualified (cut
"almost/very/really"). Show over tell. Honest over sensational — never fabricate stats or
testimonials. Remove exclamation points. Then run `anti-slop.md` for the final line-by-line pass.
